# Why you should not implant a microchip
*The surprising reason you should think twice before researching veterinary grade animal microchip implants.*

Lately several of my colleagues have expressed an interest in implantable technology. The appeal is obvious. Who doesn't want to upgrade their current physical form and add features such as:
* keyless entry to secure locations
* private data storage internal to the body
* access to an upgraded identification and payment protocol using electromagnetism (no more greasy plastic rectangles)

New implants even have temperature sensors. You can finally catch up to the overheating monitoring capabilities of literally every computer you have ever touched (you are behind!). Optional enrollment in an animal, augmented human, or cyborg registration database after getting your implant is the icing on the cake.

I'm here to argue that you are probably thinking about the decision all wrong.

While the appeals of implants are obvious the drawbacks are a bit less straightforward. Let's talk about the drawbacks.

## The arguments you're considering

First let us briefly consider the arguments you are probably already considering. These arguments are things like, "needles are scary", and "I don't know where to buy a microchip." I won't be insulting and say these things don't matter. Of course they do. The reality is, though, that these objections are just your mind shying away from taking action.

Instead of taking action your mind is latching on to anything it can to be lazy. Needles are, in fact, a bit scary. That's fine. People get piercings all the time. Animals get implants all the time. A few seconds of discomfort is not a big deal. You can do this if you want.

Your mind may be stuck on sourcing appropriate microchips or deciding on a model. That's OK, it can be hard to decide on these things. The thing you don't want to do is throw up your hands and just give up. That's just your mind making an excuse. The fact that it takes work doesn't mean you don't want it.

One thing I would suggest is instead of trying to find "the best" microchip just try to find something suitable. After doing a healthy amount of research, is your top choice something that is "good enough" for your first implant? If so then anything past this point is just your mind procrastinating. Don't let bad arguments or lazyiness stop you. At least be dissuaded by higher-quality arguments.

The next set of arguments are more on the logistical side. Your implants haven't arrived yet so you have to wait. You don't have any friends with experience doing implants or giving piercings and you don't trust yourself to do the injection. These are real objections but again, your mind might be playing tricks on you. Have you asked around? Have you watched a video on how you would do it yourself, just to see?

## The real arguments

Now we get to the real arguments.

The real issue is that the microchips are designed for public interaction. That's OK, that's a reasonable goal. But once you get a few implants, you will want to put secret keys on there to make them more useful for payments and things. Of course you will, it's an obvious upgrade.

And then you'll start thinking how nice it is to have digital storage deep inside your body. You'll want to put precious things there. You'll feel compelled to put your deepest secrets electronically inside yourself. You'll still keep a copy in your head of course, you won't want to forget. This is extra. Just having extra copies of the most important things inside you, using your flesh as a temporary buffer and holding area for silicon and circuits is deeply satisfying in itself.

Then you'll try scanning with your partner. By putting specialized machinery near you, not even touching you, they will be able to access all your deepest secrets electronically. Your implants might even glow and blink brightly in attractive colors as they extract it from you. You'll look cute as you willingly submit to the scanning. Or consensually non-consent to the scanning.

None of this is the actual problem.

The real problem is that you'll end up walking around with juicy blobs of deeply personal information embedded in your body. This is great for consensual encounters. Your partners will appreciate it. But remember when I said how the microchips are designed for public interaction?

You've got to consider unwanted interactions.

Of course there are the extreme cases of people using physical force or violence to get what they want. What if someone restrains you and pulls out a scanner? Your internal electronic protocols will not be able to shut down and stop them. You might think that in these cases it won't matter if you have implants or not, the attacker is going to have their way. That's true, but you might be specifically targetted becasue of the type of data in your implants. Attractive data that's designed for play can attract the wrong kinds of attention.

There is also the quick furtive scan. You might not even notice that some of your data was transferred. Even with colorful blinking lights you might be distracted. Your implant protocol may allow the scanner to transfer data in a way that bypasses the lights.

You start looking at the world a little differently. Most people and locations are fine. But even a tiny percentage of bad actors will affect how you live. A coffee date with someone from work you haven't really talked to becomes more fraught. Are they the type to violate your consent and do things with your electronic circuits to access your data? You have to think about it.

You might think just encrypt everything with secret keys, done. But that is just not realistic. Consenting to being scanned takes extra steps now. Why bother scanning if you are already exchanging digital keys ahead of time?

The real problem is you become a desirable target for creepy data hackers, morally questionable randos, and, most disturbingly, ex-partners that are obsessed with you. You start having to walk a delicate line between being free and open with your data for having fun with new parners and locking everything down in a way that makes it all pointless.

You are not ready.

If you were all alone in the woods walking along a path, would you rather meet a bear or a man? The man might have a scanner and want access to all your data. You don't know. The bear is just a bear.
