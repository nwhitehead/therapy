# Mitochondria: The Powerhouse of the Cell

Mitochondria are membrane-bound organelles found in the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells. Often referred to as the "powerhouse of the cell," they are responsible for producing adenosine triphosphate (ATP) through the process of oxidative phosphorylation.

## Structure

Mitochondria have a distinctive double membrane structure:
- **Outer membrane**: Smooth and contains porins that allow molecules smaller than 10,000 daltons to pass freely
- **Intermembrane space**: The area between the outer and inner membranes
- **Inner membrane**: Highly folded into cristae, containing the electron transport chain and ATP synthase
- **Matrix**: The innermost compartment, containing mitochondrial DNA, ribosomes, and enzymes for the Krebs cycle

## Function

The primary function of mitochondria is ATP production through cellular respiration. This process involves several key stages:

1. **Glycolysis** (occurs in cytoplasm): Glucose is broken down to pyruvate, producing 2 ATP
2. **Krebs cycle** (in matrix): Pyruvate is further broken down, producing electron carriers (NADH, FADH2)
3. **Electron transport chain** (inner membrane): Electron carriers donate electrons, creating a proton gradient
4. **Oxidative phosphorylation**: ATP synthase uses the proton gradient to produce approximately 32-34 ATP

## Mitochondrial DNA

Unlike most cellular organelles, mitochondria contain their own circular DNA (mtDNA). This DNA encodes 37 genes, including 13 proteins involved in oxidative phosphorylation. Mitochondrial DNA is inherited maternally in most organisms.

## Cellular Regulation

Mitochondria play crucial roles beyond ATP production:
- Calcium homeostasis
- Apoptosis (programmed cell death)
- Heat production (non-shivering thermogenesis)
- Cell signaling and differentiation

Mitochondrial dysfunction is associated with numerous diseases including neurodegenerative disorders, diabetes, and aging-related conditions.
